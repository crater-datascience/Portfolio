Part 2: Modeling the data

Now that we have a more general idea of how the data should look, we can proceed to modeling the data, and see if the results back up what we think.

salary.aov <- aov(Salary~Team*POS*Years, data = data)
summary(salary.aov)

Based on this anova model, we can verify that our hypotheses that we made following our visualizations. Based on the very small p-values from our anova model, we have statistical evidence to reject the null hypotheses and say that Position, Team and Years definitely have significant impact on the mean salary of players.

This means that the true mean of shortstops is significantly different from the true mean salary of designate hitters. Furthermore, we can see that Team, Years, and Position all have an impact on one another because the p values for their interactions are also much less than the significance level of .05.

One thing that may be helpful is visualizing some of the effects via an interaction plot, so we will do that in the next part before proceeding.

title(main="Interaction between Team and Years")

As we can see from both of these plots, there is interaction between the variables since the lines on our interaction plot regularly cross each other.

Part 4: Residual Analysis

Another thing we must do when using an anova test is to examine the residuals of our model in order to determine the normality and homoscedasticity assumptions.

plot(salary.aov, which = 2)

plot(salary.aov, which=1)

From the output of these models, we can see that our normality and homoscedasticity assumptions are likely valid. There are some outliers in the data, however, this is likely due to certain players having highly individualized contracts due to being particularly outstanding players, making them distinct outliers. We can examine this further by plotting the residuals from individual categories.

Individual Category Residuals

library(ggplot2)
data$residual <- salary.aov$residuals
ggplot(data, aes(x=Team, y=residual)) +geom_point(size=2, shape=23)+
  labs(title = "Plot of residual vs Team")+theme(axis.text.x = element_text(angle = 90, vjust = 0.5, hjust=1))

ggplot(data, aes(x=POS, y=residual)) +geom_point(size=2, shape=23)+
  labs(title = "Plot of residual vs Position")+theme(axis.text.x = element_text(angle = 90, vjust = 0.5, hjust=1))

ggplot(data, aes(x=Years, y=residual)) +geom_point(size=2, shape=23)+
  labs(title = "Plots of residual vs Years")+theme(axis.text.x = element_text(angle = 90, vjust = 0.5, hjust=1))

As we can see from our individualized residual analysis, the vast majority of the data fits more inside of the normal pattern, and we were correct in assuming that a few players make up the significant outliers, so the data is normal as it pertains to the majority.
